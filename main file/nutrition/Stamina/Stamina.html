Understand the Science Behind Weight Loss

Improving stamina through diet involves a balanced intake of macronutrients—carbohydrates, proteins, and fats. Carbs are the primary energy source, proteins aid in muscle repair, and healthy fats provide sustained energy. Including complex carbs like whole grains, lean proteins such as chicken or fish, and sources of healthy fats like avocados or nuts supports stamina

Essential vitamins and minerals, proper hydration, meal timing, and post-exercise nutrition are also crucial. Individual needs vary, so personalized guidance from a dietitian may be beneficial. Consistency in following these principles alongside regular exercise is key to long-term stamina improvement.

By focusing on these principles and tailoring your diet to support energy production, muscle repair, and overall health, you can effectively increase stamina and endurance for physical activities.

Unlocking Endurance: The Power of Dietary Nitrate

Dietary nitrate, abundant in veggies like spinach and beetroot, boosts endurance by enhancing oxygen use. Nitrate converts to nitric oxide, improving blood flow and delaying fatigue during exercise. Research shows beetroot juice can enhance performance in cyclists and runners. While individual responses vary, incorporating nitrate-rich foods may naturally enhance endurance. Consulting a nutritionist can help tailor a plan for optimal benefits.

Food Items To Limit

Milk, yoghurt, buttermilk and cheese before a workout are a strict no-no. Sugar in dairy products can interfere with the digestive process.

Orange juice has a ton of natural sugars that cause blood sugar crash and leave you tired and not in the right shape for a sweat session.

Pasta is loaded with carbohydrates which can weigh you down during a workout. You shouldn’t eat anything that has high carbs before endurance activities.

Cola, soda, flavoured water and artificial sweeteners are not easily digested by the human body and can have a loosening effect on you.

Food Items You Can Easily Consume

Soluble fibers as fruits, nuts, legumes, seeds, rice bran, oats, brown rice and barley control blood sugar levels and lower cholesterol. Insoluble fibers like whole grain bread, pasta, rice, cereals, wheat bran, corn bran, vegetables, and nuts help to build up stamina.

Proteins have a higher metabolic rate than fats and help us to burn more calories. Sources of proteins are fish, poultry, eggs, milk, cheese, legumes, and nuts.

Sources of vitamin C are orange, kiwi, lemon, lime, cranberry, apple, guava, grapefruit, grapes, spinach, kale, bell peppers, tomato, broccoli, cauliflower, brussel sprouts, gooseberry, chives, basil, and thyme.

Some iron rich foods are broccoli, spinach, meat, beans, and nuts. Calcium can be obtained by consuming milk, cheese, yogurt, green leafy vegetables, and sardines.
